Welcome to the German Alphabet Learning Page!

European languages have undergone significant sound shifts throughout history. One notable event, the Great Vowel Shift, dramatically altered the pronunciation of English vowels between the 15th and 18th centuries. This shift is part of why modern English sounds so different compared to Middle English and many other European languages, which have experienced similar changes. Understanding these shifts provides invaluable insights into learning new languages like German.

Detailed Pronunciation Guide

The German alphabet contains several sounds that do not have direct equivalents in English. Here's how to pronounce some of the trickier ones:

  • ä - Pronounced similar to the 'a' in 'care'. German examples include 'Käse' (cheese).
  • ch - Two sounds depending on the letter context:
    • After 'a', 'o', 'u', 'au': Pronounced like 'Bach' or Scottish 'loch'. Example: 'Buch' (book).
    • After 'e', 'i', 'ä', 'ö', 'ü': A soft hiss, similar to the 'h' in 'huge'. Example: 'Ich' (I).
  • e - Has two main sounds:
    • Short 'e': Similar to the 'e' in 'bet'. Example: 'Bett' (bed).
    • Long 'e': Similar to the 'ay' in 'pay', but without the glide. Example: 'See' (lake).
  • ö - No direct English equivalent, but similar to the 'i' in 'bird' with rounded lips. Other languages: similar to French 'eu' in 'peur'. Example: 'Öl' (oil).
  • sch - Pronounced like the English 'sh' in 'ship'. Example: 'Schule' (school).
  • ß (Eszett or sharp S) - Represents a double 's' sound, pronounced as 's' in 'rose'. Example: 'Straße' (street).
  • v - Usually pronounced as 'f' in 'fish'. Example: 'Vogel' (bird).
  • w - Pronounced like the 'v' in 'vase'. Example: 'Wasser' (water).
  • z - Pronounced as 'ts' in 'bits'. Example: 'Zug' (train).
  • ü - No direct English equivalent; imagine saying 'ee' as in 'see', with lips rounded as for 'oo' in 'food'. Other languages: similar to French 'u' in 'rue'. Example: 'Müde' (tired).
